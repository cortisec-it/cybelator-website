7/10 Hero section — "Learn. Stay Safe. Get Help." Needs focus
What works: The three-verb structure ("Learn. Stay Safe. Get Help.") is clean, memorable, and communicates all three platform pillars in one breath. The dark aesthetic is strong and credible. The cyan accent color works well for a cybersecurity brand.
Identity problem: "Learn. Stay Safe. Get Help." is simultaneously a training headline, a public awareness headline, and a helpdesk headline. A visitor in the first 3 seconds cannot tell what this platform primarily is. The three CTAs below (Training, Cyber Awareness, Cyber Assistance) help, but they appear small relative to the hero. The platform needs a dominant primary identity — training is the revenue engine and should own the hero.
Missing trust anchor: There is no company name, no location, no credibility signal visible in the hero. "CortiSec Technologies" is not prominently visible. A student from Guwahati seeing this for the first time has no reason to trust it over a generic cybersecurity blog. One sentence like "By Cortisec Technologies, Guwahati — Northeast India's enterprise cybersecurity company" would change the conversion math significantly.
CTA gap: The primary CTA should be "Apply for the program" or "Explore the training" — not three equal-weight options. Equal-weight choices cause decision paralysis. The training CTA must be dominant; awareness and assistance should be secondary.
7.5/10 Stats bar — 11 modules, 10 topics, 20 weeks, 5,000+ Good foundation
What works: The animated stat counters are visually effective and create immediate credibility. "20 Weeks" in cyan draws the eye. The stats section anchors the training positioning well.
Consistency issue: The blueprint specified 6 months (26 weeks) and 11 modules. The screenshot shows "20 Weeks" and "10 topics." This is a factual discrepancy that will hurt credibility if a student cross-references the course details. Use the figures from the official blueprint — 26 weeks, 11 modules — and state them consistently throughout.
"5,000+" concern: If this is a new program with no graduates yet, showing 5,000+ as a stat raises immediate skepticism from any informed visitor. Students and parents will ask: 5,000 what? Alumni? Visitors? Students? If it is not graduates, label it precisely ("5,000+ community members" or simply remove it for batch 1).
7/10 20-Week program curriculum section Solid structure
What works: The tabbed phase navigation (Overview, Network Fundamentals, Threat & Containment, etc.) is the right UX pattern for this volume of content. The module accordion list with duration badges reads as enterprise-grade. This section has genuine credibility.
Too much content density: At full page width this section is extremely long — 12+ module rows visible without interaction. A visitor scanning the page loses context. The first 3–4 modules should be visible; the rest should be behind a "Show all modules" expand. This reduces visual overwhelm without hiding content from those who want it.
"NEW" badge on modules: Several modules carry a "NEW" badge. For a brand new program, labeling modules "NEW" signals that the curriculum is not yet settled. This reduces confidence. Remove "NEW" badges entirely — the whole program is new; labeling individual modules as new suggests the others are old.
Phase tabs need clearer naming: "Threat & Containment" and "Threat Containment Readiness" read as very similar from a distance. Rename phases to match the blueprint exactly: Foundation · Core Security · Operations · Readiness.
7.5/10 Pricing section — ₹35,000 / ₹45,000 / ₹12,000 Mostly correct
What works: Three-tier pricing with a highlighted "recommended" middle card is the correct psychological structure. The cyan highlight on the ₹45,000 bundle is the right choice. EMI mention is present. The roles & salary grid below pricing is excellent positioning — it immediately answers "what will I get from spending this money?"
Launch batch strategy missing: The audit previously recommended a founding cohort price of ₹22,000–₹25,000 for the first two batches. Jumping straight to ₹35,000 without any social proof, testimonials, or batch outcome data is a conversion risk in the Assam market. Consider adding a "Founding batch — limited seats" banner on the lowest tier.
Placement disclaimer not visible: The honest placement language ("we do not guarantee placement") that was specified as mandatory is not visible in the pricing section or anywhere nearby. This is a legal and trust risk. It must appear near the enrollment CTA.
Roles grid is strong: Showing SOC Analyst, Network Security Engineer, etc. with salary ranges directly below pricing is excellent conversion psychology. A student immediately maps "₹35,000 investment → ₹4–7 LPA potential." Keep this exactly as-is.
5.5/10 10 Vendor certifications section Repositioning needed
Section identity problem: Placing a full certification catalogue grid prominently on the homepage — with individual pricing for 9 separate certifications — makes the platform look exactly like a certification coaching center. This directly contradicts the "enterprise operations, not cert mill" positioning that the entire program is built around.
Information overload: Nine certification cards with dual pricing (training fee + exam fee) is too much detail for a homepage. This is catalogue-page content, not homepage content. A visitor trying to understand whether to enroll in the 6-month program is now confused about whether they should just take a CCNA instead.
Recommendation: Replace this section on the homepage with a single compact "Additional certifications" row showing vendor logos only — Cisco, Check Point, Fortinet, CyberArk, SolarWinds — with a "View full catalogue" link to a dedicated certifications page. This preserves the certification offering without making it the homepage's identity.
4.5/10 Current threats & safety advisories section Move off homepage
Homepage identity damage: The "Current Threats & Safety Advisories" section with red/amber "CRITICAL" and "HIGH" severity badges makes the page look like a threat intelligence dashboard, not a training program or awareness platform. A student landing on this page sees ransomware alerts and phishing warnings alongside enrollment CTAs — the emotional registers are completely incompatible.
Maintenance burden: Real threat advisories require constant updating. Static or outdated threat cards ("WhatsApp scam X — last updated 3 months ago") will damage trust faster than not having them. This section has high operational cost and low conversion value on the homepage.
Where it belongs: Move to a dedicated "Threat Advisories" page linked from the Cyber Awareness section. On the homepage, replace with a single clean "Latest advisory" banner — one line, one link — that auto-pulls the most recent card. This keeps the awareness positioning without dominating the page.
7/10 Victim support & reporting guide section Emotionally strong
What works: The guided flow UI with step cards and the "Frequently Asked Questions" below it is the right UX for a victim-assistance section. The cyan "1930" helpline number visible in the interface is exactly the right trust signal for this audience. The tone appears calm and structured.
Positioning on homepage: This section sitting directly below the threat advisory section — which uses red CRITICAL badges — creates emotional whiplash. A scam victim scrolling past ransomware alerts before reaching the help section will feel more anxious, not more supported. The assistance section needs calmer visual context around it.
Voluntary service disclaimer visibility: The "Cortisec does not store your information" disclaimer must be immediately visible above the interactive flow — not buried in a footer. This is the single most important trust signal for a cybercrime victim who is scared to share any information.
FAQ section: The FAQ accordion below the reporting flow is a strong addition. Answers to "Will police contact me if I report?" and "Is my information safe?" will dramatically reduce drop-off from anxious users. Ensure these are answered directly and reassuringly.
6/10 Enrol, enquire, or get help — contact section Needs clarity
Three-audience problem: A single contact section trying to serve students enrolling, people enquiring about certifications, AND cybercrime victims seeking help is asking too much of one form. Each audience has a completely different emotional state and information need. A student is excited; a victim is scared. They should not land in the same form.
Recommendation: Split into two clear paths at the top of the section: "I want to learn cybersecurity →" (leads to enrollment form) and "I need help with a cybercrime →" (leads directly to the assistance flow). This simple fork improves both conversion and emotional safety.
No trust signals visible: The contact section needs: Cortisec's physical address, phone number, a real email, and if possible a WhatsApp link. In the Assam market, students and parents will call before they fill a form. A visible phone number in the contact section is a higher-conversion signal than any form optimization.

Overall strategic assessment
Current identity: The website currently feels 60% training institute, 25% threat intelligence dashboard, 15% victim helpdesk. The awareness positioning is almost invisible and the enterprise cybersecurity company identity is absent.
Target identity: 50% enterprise cybersecurity training program, 25% public awareness platform, 25% cyber assistance service — with Cortisec Technologies as the credible institution behind all three.
The foundation is solid. The design quality, the three-vertical concept, and the module structure are genuinely strong. The fixes needed are mostly structural and content-level — not a full redesign. With the top 5 critical fixes applied, this platform is ready for its first batch launch.
